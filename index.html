Protecting Calves by Vaccinating Cows

An overview of using dam vaccination to prevent neonatal calf scours caused by Rotavirus and Coronavirus, a cornerstone of modern calf rearing in New Zealand.

The Core Principle

You vaccinate the cow to protect the calf. The vaccine boosts the dam's antibodies, which are then concentrated in her colostrum, providing direct protection to her newborn.

Major Cause of Scours

Rotavirus and Coronavirus are two of the most common viral causes of severe, watery scours in calves under 3 weeks of age, leading to dehydration, weight loss, and death.

A Complete System

Success relies on three pillars: Vaccination, Colostrum Management, and Hygiene. If one pillar is weak, the entire system can fail.

How The Viruses Cause Scours

Rotavirus and Coronavirus cause disease by attacking the cells that line the calf's small intestine. This damage has severe consequences for the calf's ability to absorb nutrients and water.

Destruction of the Gut Lining

The viruses specifically target and destroy the mature cells at the tips of the intestinal villi. These finger-like projections are responsible for absorbing nutrients. When they are destroyed, the gut can't do its job.

Vaccination Protocol

Correct timing of vaccination is essential to ensure that antibody levels in the colostrum are at their absolute peak when the calf is born. In New Zealand, the most common vaccine used is Rotavec¬Æ Corona.

A single dose of vaccine should be given to each pregnant cow or heifer between 3 and 12 weeks before calving is expected.

Practical Timing

For most seasonal-calving herds in New Zealand, this vaccination is conveniently done at dry-off. This ensures the entire herd is covered within the correct window before calving begins.

Annual Requirement

This is not a one-off vaccine. A booster shot is required every year for every pregnant animal to ensure colostrum is fortified for each new crop of calves.

Colostrum is Key: The 3 Q's

The vaccine creates the antibodies, but colostrum delivers them. Without excellent colostrum management, the entire vaccination effort is wasted. Follow the "3 Q's" rule for every calf.

Quality

Feed only high-quality "gold" colostrum from the first milking of a healthy, vaccinated cow. Use a Brix refractometer to test quality (aim for >22%).

Quantity

The calf needs at least 10% of its bodyweight in colostrum. For a 40kg calf, this means 4 litres in the first 12 hours.

Quickly

The calf's ability to absorb antibodies plummets after birth. The first feed must be given within 6-12 hours of life.

Maximizing Vaccine Efficacy

The vaccine is not a magic bullet. Its success depends entirely on the environment the calf is born into. Good hygiene reduces the number of pathogens the calf is exposed to, making the vaccine's job possible.

Understanding "Challenge Pressure"

Think of it as a battle. The colostrum from a vaccinated cow gives the calf a shield. Hygiene determines the size of the enemy army.
